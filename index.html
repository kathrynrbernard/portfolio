What I Do

Data Visualization

I have been interested in the data analysis and visualization pipeline for years. The process of going from a raw dataset to a clear, interesting, and compelling visual aid that communicates the results of that data is fascinating to me.

Most of my past work in data visualization has been done in R, primarily utilizing the ggplot2 package to create the visualizations and hosting them on the Shiny platform. I also have experience created static and interactive maps using ArcGIS products. I am currently exploring the world of JavaScript and D3 to enhance my technical skillset.

Environmental Conservation

I left a career in healthcare IT to pursue a master's degree in Environmental Observation and Informatics, a program that integrates environmental conservation with GIS and remote sensing. I am passionate about combining my analytical and technical knowledge with my love for the environment.

Through that program, I have worked on various environmental projects, such as quantifying urban expansion in threatened wetland ecosystems. Most recently I have applied GIS to issues related to equity in outdoor recreation.

Spatial Analysis

The Environmental Observation and Informatics M.S. program has a heavy focus on remote sensing for conservation. We applied remote sensing techniques to identify areas of deforestation and urban expansion, among other applications.

My capstone project for my master's (summer 2023) involved completing a spatial analysis of the equity of access to several federal protected areas across the United States. This project involves using network analysis tools to assess the sociodemographic composition of census tracts around these locations.

A Framework for Using GIS to Assess Equitable Recreation

GIS

Link to ArcGIS Hub site

Python scripts on GitHub

Public lands are spaces that have often been restricted, both explicitly and implicitly, to individuals with racial, economic, gender, able-bodied, neurotypical, and geographic privilege. Despite the many benefits of green space and public lands for individual and community health, access to these spaces is highly variable across the United States.

As the capstone project for my master's degree, I partnered with Outdoor Alliance, a non-profit coalition of outdoor recreation advocacy groups that works at the federal policy level to advance legislation benefitting outdoor recreation and conservation. When Outdoor Alliance works to protect a place or advance an equitable access policy, they need to assess who benefits from the policy or proposal and who does not. In this project, I used geospatial methods to assess equitable recreation access. This hub site contains all the data sourcess, methodology notes, Python scripts, and visualizations I completed over the course of that project.

The goal is that the site provides enough information that someone else could reproduce this analysis on an area they are interested in - so give it a try for the lands where you live, play, or work!

  • Completion date: August 2023
  • Course: Capstone Project
  • Technologies: ArcGIS Pro (Network Analyst, Spatial Analyst), ArcGIS Online, Python (arcpy, numpy, pandas)

Network Analysis around the San Gabriel National Monument

GIS

Explore the StoryMap

This StoryMap contains the most important results of the analysis I conducted regarding equitable recreation access in the San Gabriel Mountains National Monument area. The population within a 30 mile drive of the monument is predominantly Hispanic/Latino, at a much higher proportion than the overall population of California. Communities of color systematically lack access to green space and opportunities to recreate in nature, so the fact that this population lives so close to a national monument is an opportunity to engage with them and promote their use of the monument. However, the recreational resources currently available at the monument don't align well with this group's typical recreational preferences. Research shows that Hispanic and Latino communities typically visit natural areas with a large group of extended family and use this time to strengthen their social and communal bonds, so they prefer to utilize large day use areas.

The San Gabriel Mountains, Rivers, and Foothills Protection Act would add a significant amount of acreage to the monument and, in doing so, more than double the amount of picnic areas in the monument. Increasing the number of picnic and other day use areas could encourage the local population to visit the monument more often and make use of its natural resources, which is a strong argument in favor of this legislation.

  • Course: Capstone Project
  • Technologies: ArcGIS Pro (Network Analyst, Spatial Analyst), ArcGIS Online, Python (arcpy, numpy, pandas), StoryMaps

Quantifying Greenness in Madison, WI

Remote sensing

Download Report

Google Colaboratory script link

Urban green space has numerous health-promoting benefits for city residents. Parks and other large open green spaces encourage recreation and physical activity, which has been shown to improve both physical and mental health. Other types of vegetation, such as trees planted throughout a city, contribute significantly to reducing the urban heat island effect and cooling urban areas during hot periods. There is an existing body of work dedicated to assessing the number and distribution of parks throughout cities across the United States, but less work has been done to quantify the overall greenness of cities. The present analysis quantifies the amount of green space – parks, trees, shrubs, and other green vegetation – in the city of Madison, Wisconsin.

Urban Expansion around Everglades National Park

Remote sensing

Download Report

Google Earth Engine script link

Southern Florida has been experiencing sustained population growth and urban expansion for decades. This region of the state is also home to a valuable, yet fragile, ecosystem: the Everglades. The Everglades is a large freshwater wetland that provides critical habitat for a number of threatened and endangered species. The urban growth in this area is at least partly responsible for a variety of environmental issues, including loss of biodiversity and poor water quality in the Greater Everglades region. Remote sensing and geospatial technology provide an important tool that can be used in the analysis of land cover change, and particularly urban growth, in this area. This paper assesses changes in urban areas within a 30-mile radius of Everglades National Park and Big Cypress National Preserve from 2000 to 2020 using Landsat satellite images. Change detection analysis shows that 164,040 acres of land within this radius were developed and converted to urban areas during this time frame. Information on the past two decades of urban growth can be used to inform projections of future growth and assist natural resource planners in their work to restore the water quality and health of the Greater Everglades region.

  • Completion date: December 2022
  • Course: Remote Sensing and Digital Image Processing (ES 556)
  • Technologies: Google Earth Engine, ArcGIS Pro

Land Cover Classification around Madison, WI

Remote sensing

Supervised classification is a type of classification that involves a human analyst specifying examples of each class in the training data based on the analyst’s expert knowledge. The algorithm then classifies the rest of the data based on those training examples by comparing each pixel in the full data set to the training samples and labeling the unknown pixels according to similarity. Maximum likelihood estimation is one type of supervised classification that uses decision rules based on probabilities. The algorithm calculates the probability that each pixel belongs to each class, and the pixel is assigned to the class with the highest probability.

Download Report

Maximum likelihood is a widely used algorithm and tends to be accurate. It also takes more variables into account than other methods of classification. However, this algorithm assumes class samples are normally distributed, which might not always be the case. It is also computationally intensive because of the need to calculate a co-variance matrix, it is non-spatial (meaning that it doesn’t consider where each pixel is located in the larger context of the full image), and it tends to overclassify signatures with large values in the covariance matrix (meaning that if a class has a lot of variability, many unknown pixels will end up in that class).

Support vector machines (SVM) are another type of supervised classification algorithm. They are a linear classifier derived from statistical learning theory. The goal of this algorithm is to find the optimal hyperplane that separates the classes in the data. The algorithm calculates multiple “support vectors,” or lines through the data that separate classes, and then finds the line that maximizes the distance between all data points and the vector. This line becomes the optimal hyperplane that is used to classify the remainder of the data. SVM can also be extended to non-linear data by using kernal functions to transform training data into a higher dimensional feature space. SVM is traditionally a binary classifier. It can use a one vs. all other classes approach (comparing cropland to all other classes, then forest to all other classes, etc.) or a one vs. one approach for all class pairs (comparing forest to water, forest to cropland, water to cropland, etc.). This type of pairwise comparison can become very computationally intensive as the number of classes increases, so it is typically best to keep the classes broad and use hierarchical classification to get more granular classifications. For this reason, SVM is a good classifier to use with a small amount of training data, but it becomes more difficult with larger amounts of data. Unlike most other classifiers, adding more training data does not necessarily improve the output of the algorithm. Once the support vectors are calculated, additional training data is not used.

  • Technologies: ENVI Pro
